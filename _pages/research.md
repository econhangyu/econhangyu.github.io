A central premise of my work is that poverty is shaped not only by material scarcity, but also by systematic frictions in decision-making and program delivery. On the demand side, I study how households respond to information and incentives when beliefs about the social environment are biased or when attention and memory are limited. On the supply side, I examine how programs perform in real delivery systems, focusing on what is communicated, how reliably services arrive, and how frontline workers are recruited, selected, and managed.

I pursue this agenda through sustained field engagement in Sub-Saharan Africa, combining randomized field experiments with theory-guided empirical analysis. Many of my studies use multi-stage, multi-arm designs to separate competing mechanisms that are difficult to distinguish with observational data alone. When existing data are insufficient, I build new measurements suited to the local institutional context, often with the help of new technology.

My research clusters around four themes:

1. **Demand for health under behavioral frictions** — how misperceived social norms, limited attention, and imperfect memory shape the uptake of health interventions such as HIV testing, social distancing, and ART adherence.
2. **Supply of health and program delivery** — how frontline personnel systems and implementation mechanisms determine whether resources translate into welfare, from PEPFAR-funded programs to community health worker hiring.
3. **Household responses to shocks and foreign aid** — how natural disasters and China-financed infrastructure investments reshape livelihoods, occupational choice, and human capital.
4. **AI and labor markets** — how large language models reshape labor demand, firm–worker matching, and the measurement of hiring decisions in resource-limited service delivery.
