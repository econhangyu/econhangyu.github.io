description: "Financial incentives are a cornerstone of development policy, yet they often underperform expectations. This multi-arm RCT with over 800 newly diagnosed HIV patients in Mozambique shows why: incentives alone raised antiretroviral therapy adherence by 10.6 percentage points, but pairing them with reminders — which reduce inattention to daily pill-taking and imperfect recall of payment eligibility — raised adherence by 24.3 percentage points. Detailed pharmacy refill-timing data validate a behavioral model in which two specific frictions, inattention and imperfect memory, dampen incentive effectiveness. The framework applies broadly: any incentive program tied to repeated, time-sensitive behaviors may be limited by these frictions and improved through complementary reminder interventions. This study extends the demand-side behavioral agenda from static belief-based barriers (stigma, misperceptions) to dynamic constraints on attention and memory that shape everyday health decisions."
