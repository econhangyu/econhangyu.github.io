description: "Low-income regions face disproportionate exposure to natural disasters, yet whether shocks generate persistent welfare losses or trigger adaptive reallocation remains an open question. This study combines satellite-based best-track data on Cyclone Idai — one of Mozambique's most destructive disasters — with longitudinal household surveys collected before and after the storm. Exploiting spatial variation in wind speed intensity for causal identification, we find that more heavily exposed households shifted away from agriculture toward non-farm activities, including small business, waged employment, and remittance-linked income, accompanied by greater use of the financial system. The research extends the broader agenda's focus on behavioral constraints and institutional capacity to settings where large exogenous shocks test the limits of household adaptation and local economic systems."
