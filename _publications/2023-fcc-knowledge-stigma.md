description: "Large development programs are typically evaluated on average effects, but the implementation mechanisms that shape outcomes are rarely unpacked. This study evaluates a major PEPFAR-funded program for orphans and vulnerable children in Mozambique using a multi-stage randomized design and finds that the program reduced HIV testing by 10.9 percentage points — the opposite of its intended effect. The negative impact arose because frontline implementation inadvertently spread HIV transmission myths and amplified stigma concerns. Crucially, mini-interventions that corrected these information distortions at endline fully restored testing rates, providing causal evidence that implementation biases are both diagnosable and repairable. This finding motivates a broader supply-side research agenda on how delivery systems and frontline personnel shape whether well-designed programs succeed or fail in practice."
