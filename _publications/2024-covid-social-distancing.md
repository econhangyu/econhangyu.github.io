description: "Building on the finding that social norms are often misperceived, this study examines how norm misperceptions shaped health behavior during the COVID-19 pandemic in Mozambique. A randomized experiment corrected households' systematic underestimation of community support for social distancing. Unlike the HIV-testing setting, where correcting misperceptions reduces a fixed psychological cost, the COVID-19 environment involves greater strategic uncertainty. A model separating free-riding from disease-risk signaling predicts that treatment effects should be concentrated in high-infection areas — and this is what the data show: distancing rose by 70 percent in the hardest-hit districts, with modest average effects elsewhere. The results clarify when correcting social misperceptions can shift health behavior and when they cannot, extending the demand-side behavioral framework from static stigma beliefs to dynamic decision-making under uncertainty."
