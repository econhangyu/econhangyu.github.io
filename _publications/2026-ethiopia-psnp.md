description: "Development programs fulfill their promise only when administrative systems deliver on time and in full — a principle this study illustrates by evaluating Ethiopia's Productive Safety Net Program, one of Africa's largest anti-poverty programs. Using original household panel data (2016–2021) and a doubly robust difference-in-differences design, we find that PSNP improved dietary diversity and asset accumulation, but seasonal food shortages persisted, partly due to recurrent delays in transfer disbursement during a period of civil conflict and administrative restructuring. The finding reinforces a central theme of this research agenda: program effectiveness depends critically on implementation capacity and delivery timing, not just program design. Complementary qualitative interviews help explain these patterns, connecting the quantitative evidence to the institutional realities on the ground."
